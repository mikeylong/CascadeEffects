## Framing Note

- The script keeps the documentary thesis and five-condition structure from the draft.
- The factual pass focused on the highest-risk claims: collapse-day winds and chronology, design history, aerodynamic mechanism, and the field's response after 1940.
- The pass deliberately softened any line that implied more certainty than the sources support, especially around the exact transition from vertical motion to torsional failure and the speed of the profession's later consensus.

## Claim Review

### 1. Collapse day, wind, and surviving footage

- Claim: the bridge collapsed on November 7, 1940, after opening only four months earlier.
  Status: supported as written.
  Evidence: [Board report](https://authors.library.caltech.edu/records/q1ehq-5e206), [ASCE](https://www.asce.org/about-civil-engineering/history-and-heritage/historic-landmarks/tacoma-narrows-bridges/)

- Claim: the collapse-day wind was not exceptional.
  Status: supported after tightening.
  Change: replaced the draft's broader "thirty-five to forty-six miles per hour, a moderate gale" phrasing with sourced observed values: 38 mph at 7:30 a.m. and about 42 mph near the east end later that morning. This is cleaner than leaning on a Beaufort label.
  Evidence: [Board report](https://authors.library.caltech.edu/records/q1ehq-5e206), [ASCE](https://www.asce.org/about-civil-engineering/history-and-heritage/historic-landmarks/tacoma-narrows-bridges/)

- Claim: the bridge had survived stronger winds before the collapse.
  Status: supported after correction.
  Change: revised "during construction" to "earlier autumn storms," because the stronger sourced statement is that Galloping Gertie had already experienced winds above 50 mph before November 7, not specifically that construction-stage winds are the correct comparison.
  Evidence: [WSDOT failure analysis](https://wsdot.wa.gov/TNBhistory/bridges-failure.htm), [Board report](https://authors.library.caltech.edu/records/q1ehq-5e206)

- Claim: the bridge twisted for about an hour before failure, and the footage still exists.
  Status: supported after tightening.
  Change: revised to "a little over an hour" and changed the eyewitness line from "walked off on his hands and knees, gripping the curb" to the better-supported version that Leonard Coatsworth crawled toward the East Tower after his car was thrown against the curb. Kept the motion-picture point because Farquharson and press photographers were filming, and the film later entered the National Film Registry.
  Evidence: [WSDOT collapse chronology](https://www.wsdot.wa.gov/tnbhistory/collapse.htm), [LOC registry entry list](https://www.loc.gov/programs/national-film-preservation-board/film-registry/complete-national-film-registry-listing/)

### 2. Design history, Eldridge, Moisseiff, and deck geometry

- Claim: Clark Eldridge's original concept used a conventional deep open truss deck and was later displaced by a Moisseiff redesign.
  Status: supported as written.
  Evidence: [WSDOT machine history](https://wsdot.wa.gov/TNBhistory/1940-bridge.htm), [ASCE](https://www.asce.org/about-civil-engineering/history-and-heritage/historic-landmarks/tacoma-narrows-bridges/)

- Claim: Moisseiff's redesign materially reduced projected cost.
  Status: supported after tightening.
  Change: kept the cost-saving point but used the sourced estimate range, roughly $11 million down to $7 million, instead of treating the savings more vaguely.
  Evidence: [WSDOT machine history](https://wsdot.wa.gov/TNBhistory/1940-bridge.htm)

- Claim: the key structural change was from Eldridge's deep open trusses to Moisseiff's solid plate girders about eight feet deep.
  Status: supported as written.
  Evidence: [Board report](https://authors.library.caltech.edu/records/q1ehq-5e206), [WSDOT machine history](https://wsdot.wa.gov/TNBhistory/1940-bridge.htm), [ASCE](https://www.asce.org/about-civil-engineering/history-and-heritage/historic-landmarks/tacoma-narrows-bridges/)

- Claim: the bridge was exceptionally slender, with a 1:72 span-to-width ratio.
  Status: supported after tightening.
  Change: rewrote the sentence to give the sourced dimensions directly, 2,800-foot center span and 39-foot width between the cables, rather than leaning on less precise aesthetic superlatives.
  Evidence: [WSDOT machine history](https://wsdot.wa.gov/TNBhistory/1940-bridge.htm), [Board report](https://authors.library.caltech.edu/records/q1ehq-5e206)

### 3. Pre-collapse motion and normalization

- Claim: the bridge moved almost immediately after opening and became known as Galloping Gertie.
  Status: supported as written.
  Evidence: [Board report](https://authors.library.caltech.edu/records/q1ehq-5e206), [WSDOT collapse chronology](https://www.wsdot.wa.gov/tnbhistory/collapse.htm)

- Claim: engineers were already studying the oscillations before the collapse and considering remedies.
  Status: supported after tightening.
  Change: made the pre-collapse engineering response more concrete by naming Farquharson and noting tie-down cables and wind deflectors rather than leaving the response generic.
  Evidence: [WSDOT collapse chronology](https://www.wsdot.wa.gov/tnbhistory/collapse.htm), [WSDOT aftermath and replacement history](https://www.wsdot.wa.gov/TNBHistory/bridges-aftermath.htm)

- Claim: the bridge's motion was normalized as spectacle.
  Status: interpretive but grounded.
  Change: preserved the analytical point, but phrased it as documentary interpretation based on the sourced facts that traffic continued, the nickname stuck, and the bridge's movement was widely observed rather than explicitly treated as a settled engineering conclusion.
  Evidence: [WSDOT collapse chronology](https://www.wsdot.wa.gov/tnbhistory/collapse.htm), [Board report](https://authors.library.caltech.edu/records/q1ehq-5e206)

### 4. Aerodynamic mechanism, theory limits, and the resonance myth

- Claim: Moisseiff's design relied on deflection theory, which was powerful for static loads but incomplete for aerodynamic instability in a bridge like Tacoma Narrows.
  Status: supported after tightening.
  Change: preserved the argument but made it more careful. The script now says the theory was powerful within its domain and that aerodynamic behavior in very flexible bridges was not yet formalized in ordinary design practice, rather than implying bridge engineers had no aerodynamic thinking at all.
  Evidence: [Board report](https://authors.library.caltech.edu/records/q1ehq-5e206), [WSDOT failure analysis](https://wsdot.wa.gov/TNBhistory/bridges-failure.htm)

- Claim: wind was mainly treated as a static load in bridge design of the period.
  Status: supported after tightening.
  Change: softened the absolute wording. The script now says engineers were highly capable with steady wind pressure and gravity loads, but lacked a mature standardized framework for dynamic aerodynamic behavior in very flexible long spans.
  Evidence: [Board report](https://authors.library.caltech.edu/records/q1ehq-5e206), [WSDOT failure analysis](https://wsdot.wa.gov/TNBhistory/bridges-failure.htm)

- Claim: the failure was not a simple textbook resonance story.
  Status: supported after tightening.
  Change: kept the anti-myth line, but reframed it as "too simple" rather than pretending all experts agree on one single final mechanism. The script now points to the stronger modern aerodynamic account involving torsional instability and feedback, not a neat one-frequency anecdote.
  Evidence: [Song et al. 2022](https://doi.org/10.1017/jfm.2022.748), [Billah and Scanlan 1991](https://physics.iitm.ac.in/~arul/PH1010/AJP000118TacomaBridge.pdf), [WSDOT failure analysis](https://wsdot.wa.gov/TNBhistory/bridges-failure.htm)

- Claim: once torsional flutter began, the bridge's motion amplified the aerodynamic forces acting on it.
  Status: supported after qualification.
  Change: the script now explicitly notes that engineers still debate some details of the transition from vertical to torsional motion, but that the standard modern explanation centers on self-excited torsional flutter and negative aerodynamic damping.
  Evidence: [Board report](https://authors.library.caltech.edu/records/q1ehq-5e206), [WSDOT failure analysis](https://wsdot.wa.gov/TNBhistory/bridges-failure.htm), [Song et al. 2022](https://doi.org/10.1017/jfm.2022.748)

### 5. Aftermath and the engineering response

- Claim: the collapse immediately changed the field.
  Status: supported after narrowing.
  Change: replaced the brittle "within a year" formulation with a sourced sequence: the March 1941 federal board report, University of Washington wind-tunnel work through the 1940s, and the suspension-bridge advisory board from 1942 to 1954.
  Evidence: [Board report](https://authors.library.caltech.edu/records/q1ehq-5e206), [WSDOT aftermath and replacement history](https://www.wsdot.wa.gov/TNBHistory/bridges-aftermath.htm), [ASCE](https://www.asce.org/about-civil-engineering/history-and-heritage/historic-landmarks/tacoma-narrows-bridges/)

- Claim: later suspension bridges were designed with much stronger aerodynamic awareness.
  Status: supported after softening.
  Change: removed the draft's universal claim that every major suspension bridge after 1940 explicitly modeled and tested aerodynamic behavior in one settled way. Replaced it with a stronger-supported statement that long-span bridge design increasingly incorporated aerodynamic stability analysis, wind-tunnel testing, deeper or more open stiffening systems, and other stability measures.
  Evidence: [ASCE](https://www.asce.org/about-civil-engineering/history-and-heritage/historic-landmarks/tacoma-narrows-bridges/), [WSDOT aftermath and replacement history](https://www.wsdot.wa.gov/TNBHistory/bridges-aftermath.htm), [WSDOT failure analysis](https://wsdot.wa.gov/TNBhistory/bridges-failure.htm)

- Claim: the 1950 replacement embodied those lessons.
  Status: supported as written.
  Evidence: [WSDOT aftermath and replacement history](https://www.wsdot.wa.gov/TNBHistory/bridges-aftermath.htm), [ASCE](https://www.asce.org/about-civil-engineering/history-and-heritage/historic-landmarks/tacoma-narrows-bridges/)

## Material Wording Changes Driven by Sourcing

- Replaced the draft's broad collapse-day wind range and Beaufort label with sourced observations of 38 mph at 7:30 a.m. and about 42 mph later that morning.
- Corrected "survived stronger winds during construction" to the better-supported "survived stronger winds in earlier autumn storms."
- Replaced the more theatrical Coatsworth line with the better-supported account that he crawled toward the East Tower after his car was thrown against the curb.
- Replaced the more absolute claim that the bridge was not evaluated on aerodynamic grounds at all with the narrower and stronger claim that dynamic aerodynamic investigation was not yet routine in suspension-bridge design.
- Added an explicit qualification that engineers still debate some details of the transition into catastrophic torsional motion.
- Reframed the popular resonance explanation as incomplete rather than pretending the modern literature removes every uncertainty.
- Replaced the "within a year" and "every major suspension bridge after 1940" claims with a sourced sequence of investigations, wind-tunnel work, and gradual professional adoption.

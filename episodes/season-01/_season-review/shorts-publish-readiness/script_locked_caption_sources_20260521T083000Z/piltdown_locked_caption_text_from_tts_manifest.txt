In 1912, fragments from a gravel pit in Sussex seemed to give British science the fossil it wanted.

A human-like skull. An ape-like jaw. A famous name: Piltdown Man.

The evidence was thin, but the story was powerful. It fit a brain-first theory, and gave Britain its own ancient ancestor.

Some anatomists doubted the skull and jaw belonged together. But access was limited, prestige was heavy, and the specimen kept circulating as authority.

The fraud was not perfect. It was welcome.

By 1953, tests exposed the trick. The skull was recent human bone. The jaw was from an orangutan. The teeth had been filed. The bones had been stained.

The hoax survived because it let the field feel correct.

The mystery fades. The receipts remain.

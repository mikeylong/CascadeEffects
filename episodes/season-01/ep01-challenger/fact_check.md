## Framing Note

- The script's core thesis is supported: Challenger was not only a failed part, but a long organizational failure in how risk signals were processed.
- The factual pass focused on the highest-risk claims: Shuttle cadence and schedule pressure, O-ring history, the January 27 teleconference, launch-day temperature and failure chronology, and the distinction between the Commission's findings and Diane Vaughan's later sociological framing.
- Several lines were tightened where the current draft states forecast temperatures or causal sequence more strongly than the 1986 record supports.

## Claim Review

### 1. Shuttle routine, cadence, and schedule pressure

- Claim: the Shuttle was sold as a reusable, routine system rather than a one-off expedition vehicle.
  Status: supported as written.
  Evidence: [Commission report, Volume 1](https://ntrs.nasa.gov/archive/nasa/casi.ntrs.nasa.gov/19860015255.pdf)

- Claim: NASA was operating under aggressive cadence pressure by Challenger.
  Status: supported after tightening.
  Change: revised the draft's "launches roughly every two weeks" language to a cleaner, sourced formulation: NASA was pursuing a two-flights-a-month program rhythm, had 15 flights scheduled for 1986, and had logistics plans built around 12- and 24-flight annual rates.
  Evidence: [Chapter VIII](https://www.nasa.gov/history/rogersrep/v1ch8.htm), [Commission report, Volume 1](https://ntrs.nasa.gov/archive/nasa/casi.ntrs.nasa.gov/19860015255.pdf)

### 2. O-ring design, repeated distress, and accepted risk

- Claim: the booster field joints used primary and secondary O-rings, and the design could fail if sealing did not occur in time.
  Status: supported as written.
  Evidence: [Chapter VI](https://www.nasa.gov/history/rogersrep/v1ch6.htm), [Commission report, Volume 1](https://ntrs.nasa.gov/archive/nasa/casi.ntrs.nasa.gov/19860015255.pdf)

- Claim: O-ring erosion and blow-by had been observed repeatedly before Challenger, and management gradually treated the problem as acceptable.
  Status: supported as written.
  Evidence: [Chapter VI](https://www.nasa.gov/history/rogersrep/v1ch6.htm)

- Claim: surviving flights were repeatedly read as evidence that the system could tolerate the anomaly.
  Status: interpretive but strongly grounded.
  Change: preserved this thesis because the Commission explicitly found that NASA and Thiokol increased the amount of damage considered acceptable instead of grounding or redesigning the joint.
  Evidence: [Chapter VI](https://www.nasa.gov/history/rogersrep/v1ch6.htm)

### 3. January 27 teleconference, temperature, and the 53F recommendation

- Claim: Thiokol engineers argued against launch on the night of January 27, 1986.
  Status: supported as written.
  Evidence: [Volume IV, Part 3](https://www.nasa.gov/history/rogersrep/v4part3.htm), [Volume IV, Part 7](https://www.nasa.gov/history/rogersrep/v4part7.htm)

- Claim: the engineering recommendation was not to launch below 53 degrees.
  Status: supported after tightening.
  Change: revised the line so it is clear that 53F was the lowest prior O-ring experience base and calculated O-ring-area temperature for the coldest prior flight, not a simple long-standing ambient pad launch criterion.
  Evidence: [Volume IV, Part 3](https://www.nasa.gov/history/rogersrep/v4part3.htm), [Volume IV, Part 7](https://www.nasa.gov/history/rogersrep/v4part7.htm)

- Claim: the script's exact "18 degrees at the pad" forecast line is the best-supported version of the weather record.
  Status: too strong as written and revised.
  Change: replaced the exact pad-forecast claim with a more defensible formulation: forecasts and engineering temperature estimates pointed to conditions well below prior launch experience, with overnight pad forecasts in the low twenties and some engineering discussion citing values as low as 18F.
  Evidence: [Volume IV, Part 5](https://www.nasa.gov/history/rogersrep/v4part5.htm), [Volume IV, Part 3](https://www.nasa.gov/history/rogersrep/v4part3.htm)

- Claim: the recommendation changed after management pushback and without new evidence.
  Status: supported after qualification.
  Change: kept the core point. The historical record supports strong management pushback, a caucus, and a reversal to a launch recommendation; "without new evidence" is defensible as documentary interpretation because the record emphasizes debate over the same data rather than a new technical discovery.
  Evidence: [Volume IV, Part 7](https://www.nasa.gov/history/rogersrep/v4part7.htm), [Commission report, Volume 1](https://ntrs.nasa.gov/archive/nasa/casi.ntrs.nasa.gov/19860015255.pdf)

### 4. Launch-day temperature and failure chronology

- Claim: Challenger launched at 11:38 a.m. Eastern on January 28, 1986, at 36F, colder than any previous Shuttle launch.
  Status: supported as written.
  Evidence: [Commission report, Volume 1](https://ntrs.nasa.gov/archive/nasa/casi.ntrs.nasa.gov/19860015255.pdf), [Columbia Magazine summary of the launch conditions](https://www.magazine.columbia.edu/article/challenger-disaster-normalization-deviance)

- Claim: smoke appeared in the first second after ignition and indicated early O-ring seal failure.
  Status: supported as written.
  Evidence: [Commission report, Volume 1](https://ntrs.nasa.gov/archive/nasa/casi.ntrs.nasa.gov/19860015255.pdf)

- Claim: wind shear definitively broke the temporary seal at exactly 59 seconds.
  Status: too categorical as written and revised.
  Change: revised to the stronger-supported version: at about 58 to 59 seconds the leak became visibly evident again as flame, and the Commission said wind shear, thrust vectoring, and vehicle motion may have reinitiated or magnified leakage from a degraded seal.
  Evidence: [Commission report, Volume 1](https://ntrs.nasa.gov/archive/nasa/casi.ntrs.nasa.gov/19860015255.pdf)

- Claim: the vehicle broke apart at 73 seconds.
  Status: supported as written.
  Evidence: [Commission report, Volume 1](https://ntrs.nasa.gov/archive/nasa/casi.ntrs.nasa.gov/19860015255.pdf)

### 5. Commission findings, Feynman, and later sociological framing

- Claim: Reagan appointed a Presidential Commission chaired by William Rogers, with Richard Feynman among its members.
  Status: supported as written.
  Evidence: [Commission report, Volume 1](https://ntrs.nasa.gov/archive/nasa/casi.ntrs.nasa.gov/19860015255.pdf)

- Claim: the Commission found a flawed launch decision process, communication failures, and management structures that allowed safety problems to bypass key managers.
  Status: supported as written.
  Evidence: [Commission report, Volume 1](https://ntrs.nasa.gov/archive/nasa/casi.ntrs.nasa.gov/19860015255.pdf)

- Claim: Diane Vaughan later named the larger pattern "normalization of deviance."
  Status: supported after clarification.
  Change: retained the line but treat it explicitly as later sociological framing, not as language used by the 1986 Commission itself.
  Evidence: [Columbia Magazine](https://www.magazine.columbia.edu/article/challenger-disaster-normalization-deviance)

## Material Wording Changes Driven by Sourcing

- Replaced the draft's "launches roughly every two weeks" line with a sourced two-flights-a-month / 15-flights-in-1986 formulation.
- Replaced the brittle "18 degrees at the pad" forecast line with a more accurate low-twenties / as-low-as-18F framing tied to the actual hearing record.
- Reframed the 53F line so it refers to the prior O-ring experience base rather than implying a settled ambient launch rule.
- Replaced the categorical "wind shear broke the temporary seal" line with the stronger-supported "leak became visibly evident again around 58 to 59 seconds, and wind shear plus vehicle loads may have magnified or reinitiated it."
- Clarified that "normalization of deviance" is a later Vaughan framing layered onto the Commission's original findings, not the Commission's own terminology.

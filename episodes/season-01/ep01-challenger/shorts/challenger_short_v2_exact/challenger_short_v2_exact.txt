[deliberate] The night before Challenger launched, an engineer begged NASA not to fly. He had data. He had charts. He had the math. They launched anyway. And the reason why is more disturbing than the explosion itself.

[matter-of-fact] The O-rings that sealed the rocket boosters were never designed to work in cold weather. Engineers at Morton Thiokol knew this. They'd been flagging it for months. But every time a shuttle flew with damaged O-rings and nothing went wrong, the damage stopped looking like a warning and started looking like proof that the system worked. NASA had a term for this. They called it "acceptable erosion."

[frustrated] On the night of January 27th, 1986, engineer Roger Boisjoly told NASA the forecast was too cold. The O-rings would fail. NASA asked Thiokol to prove it was unsafe to fly. Not prove it was safe. Prove it was unsafe. That's an inversion that changes everything because in engineering, you qualify a system for launch. You don't launch by default and wait for someone to stop you.

[flatly] But by 1986, launching was the default. Forty-one seconds after liftoff, hot gas burned through the O-ring exactly where Boisjoly said it would. Seventy-three seconds in, the shuttle broke apart. Seven people died not because one person made a bad call, but because an entire system had learned to look at damage and call it normal.

[deliberate] Small causes. Massive consequences.

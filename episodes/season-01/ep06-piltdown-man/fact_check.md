## Framing Note

- The script keeps the draft's institutional-failure thesis and five-condition structure.
- The factual pass focused on the highest-risk claims: the 1912 announcement context, the 1949 versus 1953 testing timeline, the early skeptical response, the "brain first" expectation, and the evidence tying the fraud most strongly to Dawson.
- The pass tightened a few claims that were rhetorically strong but historically sloppier than the sources support.

## Claim Review

### 1. Discovery, institution name, and initial reception

- Claim: the find was announced in December 1912 and presented as a human-ape "missing link."
  Status: supported as written after historical naming and voiceover cleanup.
  Change: replaced repeated uses of the formal historical name "British Museum (Natural History)" with a smoother first-reference phrase, "the London museum we now call the Natural History Museum," then "the museum" after that. The formal 1912 name remains historically important, but it reads awkwardly in narration. The NHM institutional history confirms the museum was not officially renamed the Natural History Museum until 1992, and the NHM archive page confirms the 1912 Geological Society announcement and the basic composition of the find.
  Evidence: [NHM collection page](https://www.nhm.ac.uk/our-science/services/library/collections/piltdown-man.html), [NHM institutional history](https://www.nhm.ac.uk/about-us/history-and-architecture.html), [De Groote et al. 2016](https://discovery.ucl.ac.uk/id/eprint/1508682/)

- Claim: the specimen fit a "brain first" theory of human evolution and was especially attractive to the British scientific establishment.
  Status: supported after tightening.
  Change: kept the national and theoretical-expectation argument, but framed it as reception context rather than as a monocausal explanation. The open-access Branch and Scott review explicitly ties Piltdown's acceptance to the brain-first view and notes a role for national pride.
  Evidence: [Branch and Scott 2013](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/1936-6434-6-27)

- Claim: the specimen quickly became an authoritative part of mainstream scientific discussion.
  Status: supported after narrowing.
  Change: trimmed the draft's stronger museum-display wording to a cleaner claim about endorsement, publication, and wide cast circulation. Arthur Keith's 1913 Nature letter explicitly refers to casts already being "in circulation."
  Evidence: [Keith 1913](https://www.nature.com/articles/092292a0), [NHM collection page](https://www.nhm.ac.uk/our-science/services/library/collections/piltdown-man.html)

### 2. Early skepticism and why it did not dislodge consensus

- Claim: skepticism began almost immediately, especially around whether the skull and jaw belonged together.
  Status: supported as written.
  Change: preserved the point and named it more clearly as an anatomical objection. NHM's research summary notes that some anatomists doubted from the beginning, and the Branch and Scott review notes that early critics questioned whether the jaw belonged to the skull.
  Evidence: [NHM research summary](https://www.nhm.ac.uk/discover/news/2016/august/piltdown-man-charles-dawson-likely-fraudster.html), [Branch and Scott 2013](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/1936-6434-6-27)

- Claim: institutional prestige made skepticism costly and helped insulate the find.
  Status: interpretive but grounded.
  Change: left this as documentary analysis rather than pretending there is a single source that proves motive or institutional psychology. It is consistent with the documented prestige of Woodward and the museum, the constrained access to originals, and the review literature on Piltdown's reception.
  Evidence: [NHM research summary](https://www.nhm.ac.uk/discover/news/2016/august/piltdown-man-charles-dawson-likely-fraudster.html), [Branch and Scott 2013](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/1936-6434-6-27)

### 3. Dating and forensic exposure timeline

- Claim: fluorine testing was central to the exposure, but the decisive case was assembled in the early 1950s rather than appearing in a single instant.
  Status: supported after correction.
  Change: changed the opening fraud-exposure line from "In 1953, fluorine dating..." to "By 1953..." because the fluorine work began in 1949 and was published in 1950, while the 1953 bulletin synthesized fluorine evidence with microscopy and X-ray analysis.
  Evidence: [Oakley and Hoskins 1950](https://www.nature.com/articles/165379a0), [Weiner, Oakley, and Le Gros Clark 1953](https://www.biodiversitylibrary.org/part/313845), [NHM collection page](https://www.nhm.ac.uk/our-science/services/library/collections/piltdown-man.html)

- Claim: the remains were a composite of human and orangutan material, with filed teeth and artificial staining.
  Status: supported as written.
  Change: kept the core fraud description. NHM's archive page states that the skull and jaw came from different species, that tooth scratches showed filing, and that most finds were artificially stained to match the local gravels.
  Evidence: [NHM collection page](https://www.nhm.ac.uk/our-science/services/library/collections/piltdown-man.html)

- Claim: the draft's mention of flint tools showing marks from modern metal tools was necessary to the episode.
  Status: removed.
  Change: cut this line. It appears in the longer 1955 follow-up literature, but it was not necessary for the core fraud argument and was not worth carrying as a higher-friction detail when the skull-jaw composite, filed teeth, and staining are already sufficient and better-attested.

### 4. Dawson and the question of authorship

- Claim: Dawson is the primary suspect and modern analysis points strongly toward him as the likely sole forger.
  Status: supported after tightening.
  Change: kept the wording at "pointed strongly" rather than "proved." The 2016 study links all forged material to a consistent method and argues that Dawson is the likeliest single perpetrator, but it still leaves open the possibility that he may not have acted entirely alone.
  Evidence: [De Groote et al. 2016](https://discovery.ucl.ac.uk/id/eprint/1508682/), [NHM research summary](https://www.nhm.ac.uk/discover/news/2016/august/piltdown-man-charles-dawson-likely-fraudster.html)

### 5. Teaching, authority, and the episode's thesis

- Claim: Piltdown was repeated through teaching and other authoritative channels for decades.
  Status: supported at a high level, but treated as background rather than the evidentiary core.
  Change: kept the line in restrained form. The strongest directly accessible source in this pass is the Bergman review abstract noting that Piltdown was touted for decades by textbooks, articles, and major museums. I treated that as contextual support, not as a point the episode depends on sentence by sentence.
  Evidence: [Bergman 2003 abstract](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/15055883/)

- Claim: the script's larger lesson is that institutions can fail by applying their normal standards inside a framework that is not designed to detect a particular kind of fraud.
  Status: interpretive but well grounded.
  Change: preserved this thesis. It is not a direct quotation from the sources; it is an inference from the documented pattern of expectations, prestige, restricted scrutiny, and delayed re-examination.

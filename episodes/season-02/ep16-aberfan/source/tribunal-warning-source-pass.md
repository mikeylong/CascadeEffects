## Strong Enough For Narration

### 1. The event and human consequence

Safe claim:

On October 21, 1966, a National Coal Board spoil tip above Aberfan collapsed and engulfed Pantglas Junior School and nearby homes. 144 people died, including 116 children.

Why it is safe:

This is supported by the existing source audit, the government inquiry digest context, and the ICE summary. It should be used once early and then handled with restraint. The script should not keep returning to casualty arithmetic for emotional force.

### 2. The object was industrial, not natural

Safe claim:

The fatal material was colliery spoil: mining waste placed above the village. Rain and water mattered, but the hazard was not a natural hillside failure. It was an industrial waste structure in a water-sensitive place.

Why it is safe:

The ICE source defines the spoil tip as waste from coal mining and says the failure involved tipped coal mixed with water. The existing audit also warns against natural-disaster framing. This is the cleanest way to keep the mechanism visible without overexplaining geology.

### 3. Water and placement are narration-safe

Safe claim:

The hillside above Aberfan had springs and streams, and the failed tip interacted with water and drainage conditions. The script can say the danger was not only that the tip was large; it was where and how the waste was allowed to sit.

Why it is safe:

The ICE account says the hillside was riddled with springs, streams were buried beneath the tips, Tip 7 covered material that had previously slipped in 1944, and the catastrophic failure resulted from water building up in the tip. The writer packet's physical layer matches this.

Use this as the mechanism's physical spine:

- spoil above village
- water/spring/drainage conditions
- prior instability in the same tip landscape
- failure path toward school and homes

### 4. Prior instability is narration-safe, but avoid overspecific certainty without a direct tribunal cite

Safe claim:

There had been earlier tip instability in the Aberfan tip complex before 1966, including a major 1944 failure referenced by ICE. Prior slips and ground movement belong in the warning record.

Handle cautiously:

Do not narrate a detailed chronology of every slip, named official, or exact internal exchange unless the later fact-check pass opens the tribunal report text directly. The review-ready draft can say "there had been earlier failures and movements in this landscape" and tie that to institutional non-urgency.

### 5. Local concern is narration-safe as a category, not yet as a scene

Safe claim:

Concerns were raised before the disaster, and the tribunal/government framing supports "failure to heed clear warnings" as an official conclusion.

Why it is safe:

The government inquiry digest quotes the tribunal's conclusion as describing failure to heed clear warnings and a lack of direction from above. ICE says concerns were raised and no action was taken before it was too late.

Handle cautiously:

Do not invent a local complaint scene. Do not put a resident, parent, teacher, miner, council member, or official into dialogue unless a direct source is in hand. The emotional center should come from the visible relationship between the tip, the village, and the school, not from reconstructed conversations.

### 6. National Coal Board responsibility is narration-safe

Safe claim:

The tribunal placed responsibility on the National Coal Board, and the government inquiry digest says Lord Robens admitted the Coal Board had been at fault near the end of the hearings.

Why it is safe:

This is supported by the government inquiry digest and the existing source audit. It is central to the accountability sequence.

Handle cautiously:

Keep the tone institutional, not villain-first. The tribunal language supports blame and preventability, but the episode should focus on how ownership failed to become urgency.

### 7. Absence of policy/regulatory coverage is narration-safe

Safe claim:

Before Aberfan, tip safety sat in a weak policy and regulatory environment. ICE says there was an absence of UK laws and regulations governing mine and quarry waste tips, and that the NCB had no tipping policy that would have enforced appropriate design and management.

Why it is safe:

This supports the systems frame: the visible risk remained institutionally under-specified until disaster forced the category into law and management.

Use cautiously:

Do not imply that a single statute alone would have prevented Aberfan. Treat law and policy as part of the delayed recognition, not as a magic fix.

### 8. Post-disaster law can close the mechanism

Safe claim:

The Mines and Quarries (Tips) Act 1969 and later regulations belong in the closing section as evidence that the system eventually named tip stability as a category requiring design, inspection, and management.

Why it is safe:

ICE links the inquiry aftermath to the 1969 Act and 1971 regulations. The existing audit includes the 1969 Act as post-disaster legal response.

## What Not To Narrate Yet

- Do not narrate the National Library tribunal PDF as directly read in this pass. The URL was reachable only as a verification page.
- Do not claim exact internal NCB knowledge dates unless sourced in the later direct tribunal/fact-check pass.
- Do not make maps, plans, or diagrams the audience-facing evidence object. They can inform production, but Mike's note says the episode's emotional center should not lean on maps or documents.
- Do not turn the episode into royal response, media memory, charity-fund aftermath, or general Welsh coal history.
- Do not use "the community knew and the institution ignored them" as a single sweeping claim. Split it into visible risk, prior instability, concerns raised, weak policy, and institutional non-urgency.

## Narration-Safe Claim Set

1. Aberfan was a preventable industrial disaster, not a natural accident.
2. The hazard was visible: coal spoil had been placed above a village and school.
3. Water made the placement dangerous, because springs, streams, drainage, and saturated spoil were part of the failure mechanism.
4. Prior instability and raised concerns made the risk legible before the collapse.
5. The National Coal Board owned the risk, but ownership did not become urgent action.
6. The tribunal's accountability frame and the later tips legislation show the system recognized the category after the consequence.
7. The emotional center is not a courtroom document. It is the unbearable physical fact that the risk sat above ordinary life long enough to become normal.

## Script Implication

The draft can move forward.

The source pass is strong enough for a review-ready longform script if the script avoids invented scenes, keeps official accountability precise, and leaves detailed warning chronology for the next critique/fact-check pass. The central promise should be:

Aberfan did not happen because nobody could see the hill. It happened because a visible industrial risk was allowed to remain ordinary until it moved.

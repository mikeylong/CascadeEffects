## Episode

Aberfan Disaster.

## Primary Lane

Design Failures That Changed the World.

House question: what constraint failed, and how did the system let it fail?

## Mechanism

Visible industrial risk failed to become institutional urgency.

The episode should begin with what changed inside the system: coal waste above Aberfan was no longer just part of the mining landscape. It had become a water-sensitive hazard positioned over a village and school, while responsibility, warning signals, and urgency stayed blurred inside the institution that owned the risk.

This is not a "rain caused a landslide" story. The Cascade Effects story is how an industrial waste structure could be visible, complained about, historically unstable, and still not be converted into decisive action before the hill moved.

## Public Version

A coal tip collapsed after heavy rain and buried Pantglas Junior School and nearby homes.

## Incomplete Version

That version leaves out the mechanism. The stronger episode is about a visible hazard that failed to become a system priority: spoil placement, water and drainage conditions, prior slips, local concern, institutional ownership, and delayed recognition turned a known landscape feature into a preventable disaster.

## Evidence Base

Supported factual spine from the source audit and canonical row:

1. On October 21, 1966, a coal spoil tip collapsed above Aberfan and engulfed Pantglas Junior School and nearby homes.
2. 144 people died, including 116 children.
3. The Aberfan Tribunal placed responsibility on the National Coal Board and treated the disaster as preventable.
4. The local physical mechanism centers on coal spoil, water/drainage conditions, and the position of the tip above the village.
5. The canonical row frames the episode around institutional inertia, environmental risk ignored, local warnings, blurred ownership, and a visible hazard that did not become urgent.
6. The canonical row is reconstructed and the original 1-31 source row remains missing, so source-row confidence should stay flagged until the original starter source is recovered or formally waived.

Interpretation to test in script:

- The changed-first event is not the morning collapse. It is the earlier moment when the tip crossed from managed industrial residue into a public hazard without the institution acting as if the category had changed.
- The audience should feel the mechanism through a simple spatial question: why was unstable industrial waste still allowed to sit above a school?
- "Warnings ignored" must be built from evidence categories, not slogan: prior slips, drainage concerns, local complaints, and formal institutional knowledge should be separated before the script makes any broad claim.
- Later royal, media, and national-memory material can appear only after the causal mechanism is clear.

## Causal Layers

- Physical layer: coal spoil accumulated above Aberfan and interacted with water and drainage conditions.
- Placement layer: the village and Pantglas Junior School sat below the failure path.
- Signal layer: prior slips, drainage concerns, local complaints, and institutional knowledge need precise separation.
- Ownership layer: responsibility did not convert visible risk into urgent action before the collapse.
- Tribunal layer: the inquiry's preventability and National Coal Board responsibility should anchor the accountability sequence.
- Memory layer: later royal/media memory is context, not the episode's causal spine.

## Point Of No Return

The episode turns when the hill above Aberfan stops being treated as background mining infrastructure and becomes an unresolved institutional risk over a school.

## Compact Thesis

Aberfan shows how a disaster can come from a hazard everyone could see when ownership, evidence, and urgency failed to meet before the risk moved.

Runtime target: compact 12 to 18 minutes. Do not expand into a general history of Welsh coal mining, a royal-response episode, or a memory-politics essay.

## 10-Part Spine

1. Open on the changed category: a hill of mining waste above a school had become a hazard, but the system still treated it as scenery.
2. Establish the spatial mechanism: village, Pantglas Junior School, tip location, slope, and water path.
3. Give the public version briefly: heavy rain, collapse, school buried.
4. Narrow the public version: rainfall mattered, but the tip was an industrial waste structure, not a natural hillside.
5. Explain spoil tips and why water/drainage conditions mattered without overclaiming beyond the inquiry record.
6. Build the warning record in separated lanes: prior slips, drainage concerns, local complaints, and formal institutional knowledge.
7. Show the collapse path and immediate consequence with restraint, using casualty facts sparingly and precisely.
8. Move to accountability: the tribunal's preventability finding and National Coal Board responsibility.
9. Show the system response after the fact: tip safety law and policy made the invisible ownership problem visible.
10. Close on the compact thesis: the warning hill was visible before the institution treated it as urgent.

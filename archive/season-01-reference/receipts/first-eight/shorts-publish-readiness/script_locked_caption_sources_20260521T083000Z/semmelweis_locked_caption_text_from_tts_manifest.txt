In Vienna, two maternity wards sat under one hospital roof.

The First Clinic was run by doctors and students. The Second was run by midwives. The patients were similar. The death rates were not.

By 1846, the First Clinic was losing more than one in ten mothers. Across the same years, the Second stayed under four percent.

Semmelweis found the difference in the routine. Doctors moved from autopsies to childbirth without effective disinfection.

He required chloride-of-lime hand disinfection. Mortality fell.

The evidence worked before germ theory could explain why.

That was the problem. Believing the data meant believing doctors had been carrying death from the dead to the living.

The institution protected its identity longer than it protected the evidence.

Small causes, massive consequences.

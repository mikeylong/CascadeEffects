## Claim Review

### 1. Ward mortality gap

- Claim: the two clinics operated under the same institutional roof, with physicians and medical students in the First Clinic and midwives in the Second.
  Status: supported as written.
  Evidence: [Loudon 2013](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3807776/), [Stang et al. 2022](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10654-022-00871-8)

- Claim: the First Clinic averaged about 10% mortality in 1841-1846 while the Second was closer to 2%, and the gap was fivefold year after year.
  Status: supported after softening.
  Change: revised to "about ten percent" in the First and "under four percent" in the Second over the six-year span, with the more precise note that 1846 was about fivefold and the six-year average was about threefold.
  Evidence: [Semmelweis 1861 excerpt](https://www.jameslindlibrary.org/semmelweis-i-1861/), [Loudon 2013](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3807776/)

- Claim: women feared the First Clinic and "street births" had lower mortality.
  Status: supported after softening.
  Change: kept the fear/street-birth point but removed the more theatrical wording about begging and preferring outdoor birth, because the cleaner sourced claim is that women feared Clinic 1 and Semmelweis cited lower mortality among street births.
  Evidence: [Stang et al. 2022](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10654-022-00871-8)

- Claim: priest-bell and similar explanations were considered and tested.
  Status: supported as written.
  Evidence: [Ravel et al. 2024](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12720402/), [Kadar et al. revisit via PMC12103370 summary](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12103370/)

### 2. Kolletschka and the autopsy link

- Claim: Jakob Kolletschka's death in March 1847 triggered Semmelweis's breakthrough.
  Status: supported as written.
  Evidence: [Best et al. 2010](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC2870773/), [Stang et al. 2022](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10654-022-00871-8)

- Claim: Kolletschka died after a scalpel wound during autopsy and his pathological findings resembled fatal puerperal fever.
  Status: supported after softening.
  Change: revised "identical" to "closely resembled" to stay closer to the historical wording.
  Evidence: [Best et al. 2010](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC2870773/), [Ravel et al. 2024](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12720402/)

- Claim: First Clinic staff moved from autopsies to laboring women, while Second Clinic midwives did not perform autopsies.
  Status: supported as written.
  Evidence: [Loudon 2013](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3807776/), [Best et al. 2010](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC2870773/)

### 3. Chloride-of-lime intervention

- Claim: Semmelweis introduced chloride-of-lime hand disinfection in May 1847.
  Status: supported as written.
  Evidence: [Loudon 2013](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3807776/), [Best et al. 2010](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC2870773/)

- Claim: mortality dropped from roughly ten percent to just over one percent within a season.
  Status: supported after softening.
  Change: revised to the stronger-supported version: mortality fell sharply within weeks, and by 1848 the First Clinic's annual mortality was about 1.3%, essentially equal to the Second Clinic.
  Evidence: [Loudon 2013](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3807776/), [Stang et al. 2022](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10654-022-00871-8), [Science History Institute](https://www.sciencehistory.org/education/scientific-biographies/ignaz-semmelweis/)

- Claim: ordinary soap-and-water washing was the key intervention.
  Status: not solid enough as written and reframed.
  Change: the script now explicitly says chloride-of-lime or chlorinated-lime disinfection, not generic handwashing with plain soap and water.
  Evidence: [Loudon 2013](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3807776/), [Semmelweis University](https://semmelweis.hu/english/about-semmelweis-university/semmelweis200/)

### 4. Resistance, publication, and death

- Claim: the medical establishment rejected Semmelweis outright.
  Status: supported after softening.
  Change: revised to "much of the medical establishment" because he did have allies and supporters in Vienna and beyond.
  Evidence: [Semmelweis University](https://semmelweis.hu/english/about-semmelweis-university/semmelweis200/), [Science History Institute](https://www.sciencehistory.org/education/scientific-biographies/ignaz-semmelweis/)

- Claim: Semmelweis later wrote open letters accusing colleagues of causing avoidable deaths.
  Status: supported as written.
  Evidence: [Semmelweis University](https://semmelweis.hu/english/about-semmelweis-university/semmelweis200/), [Science History Institute](https://www.sciencehistory.org/education/scientific-biographies/ignaz-semmelweis/)

- Claim: he died in an asylum from the same infection he spent his life trying to prevent.
  Status: supported after softening.
  Change: revised to say that contemporary records attribute his death to sepsis after a wound to his hand, while the precise circumstances remain disputed.
  Evidence: [Semmelweis University](https://semmelweis.hu/english/about-semmelweis-university/semmelweis200/), [Wilkinson 2021](https://doi.org/10.1192/bjp.2020.238), [Loudon 2013](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3807776/)

### 5. Germ theory and later acceptance

- Claim: Semmelweis lacked an accepted explanatory framework because germ theory was not yet established.
  Status: supported as written.
  Evidence: [Loudon 2013](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3807776/), [Stang et al. 2022](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10654-022-00871-8)

- Claim: by 1880 the profession had already broadly accepted Semmelweis's intervention in the simple form stated in the draft.
  Status: supported after softening.
  Change: revised to "by the 1880s, after Pasteur's work on germs and Listerian antisepsis, antiseptic practice was becoming routine in obstetrics." This is historically stronger than claiming a clean, single-date vindication.
  Evidence: [Loudon 2013](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3807776/)

## Material Wording Changes Driven by Sourcing

- Replaced the six-year "ten percent against two" claim with the better-supported "about ten percent versus under four percent."
- Replaced "five times lower" as a general summary with "fivefold in 1846, roughly threefold across the six-year span."
- Removed the unsupported "fifty feet apart" detail.
- Replaced "almost always fatal" with "often proved fatal."
- Replaced "identical" pathology language for Kolletschka with "closely resembled."
- Replaced generic "handwashing" mechanics with chloride-of-lime disinfection where the script was making a factual claim.
- Replaced "just over one percent within a season" with the better-supported 1848 annual figure of about 1.3%.
- Replaced "the medical establishment rejected him" and "the profession could not absorb this" with softer formulations acknowledging that support existed but broad resistance remained.
- Replaced the more brittle death line with a sepsis-and-dispute formulation tied to autopsy records and later historical review.
- Replaced the 1880 single-date acceptance framing with a softer 1880s bacteriology/Listerian-antisepsis framing.
